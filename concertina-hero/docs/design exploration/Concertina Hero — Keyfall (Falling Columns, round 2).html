Keyfall

Your mockup fused two of the concepts: keep the real anglo keyboard on the floor of the playfield, and drop each note straight down onto the exact button it wants. Here it is built out at 30 buttons, then four decisions worth settling.

30 buttons · 3 rows × 5 × 2 hands keyboard is the hit zone bellows split down the middle

The idea, built out

The instrument is the bottom of the screen

Both hands sit in their true staggered positions, divided by the bellows. A note falls down a coloured track and lands on its button — the top half lights for a pull, the bottom half for a push. Buttons with nothing coming stay grey, so a busy 30-button tune only ever lights the handful of buttons in play.

Decision 01

Do the empty buttons show their tracks?

Thirty faint guide lines make the field busy, but they hold every button in a fixed spatial slot so your eye learns where each one lives. Lighting only the active tracks is calmer and matches your sketch, at the cost of the field looking different every bar.

A Active only

Only buttons with an incoming note draw a track. Quietest; closest to the mockup.

B All guides

Every button trails a faint guide; active ones light up over it. Busier, but spatially stable.

Decision 02 · the crux

Where does timing happen?

Your buttons sit at different heights, so if every note lands on its own button, notes on the back row travel farther than the front row — the hit point moves around the screen. The alternative is a single strike line just above the keyboard, with the keys below as a live map of which finger to move. It keeps timing in one place but breaks the "land on the button" feeling.

A Land on the button

The button is the hit zone, wherever it sits. Truest to the instrument; timing target roams.

B Shared strike line

All notes hit one line; the keyboard below just shows which button lit. Steady timing; less physical.

Decision 03

Push and pull on one button

Each button carries two notes. Following your instrument sketch, the pull note sits on the top half and the push on the bottom. A falling pill already says its direction two ways — striped for pull, solid for push — and it lights the matching half as it lands, so the bellows direction reads before you even parse the letter.

Decision 04

What the colours mean

Your mockup used a different colour per falling note so you can track them apart. That works, but a fixed scheme teaches the layout: hand sets the temperature — left cool, right warm — and column sets the hue, five across each hand. Then a note's colour tells you which hand and roughly which finger before it lands.

A Fixed by position

Colour = hand + column. The same button is always the same colour, every song.

B Cycling per note

Colour just keeps adjacent notes distinct (your mockup). Simpler, teaches nothing.

Still open

Questions this round raises

  • The staggered-track problem. Real anglo columns aren't evenly spaced, so tracks from neighbouring buttons can sit close together. We either keep true physical spacing (authentic, occasionally cramped) or snap tracks to an even grid and let only the button shapes hint at the stagger.
  • How tall is the keyboard zone? The more room the keys take, the shorter the fall — less warning time. There's a trade between "looks like the instrument" and "I can see it coming".
  • Back-row reachability. If timing lands on the button (02·A), back-row notes need a longer track and therefore start earlier. Might actually be fine — it mirrors the extra reach — but it needs playtesting.
  • Keyboard keys. 30 buttons won't fit the number row. Two 3×5 blocks — left hand on Q-T / A-G / Z-B, right on Y-P / H-; / N-/ — mirror the on-screen hands.
