Anatomy of a hold

The head is the moment to press; the tail is the hold. Count the bands it covers and you know the length before it arrives.

How length reads at a glance

Three notes on the same button, three lengths. No numbers to read — the shape carries the rhythm, which is the whole point of a falling-note game.

Decision 04, spelled out

Colour fixed by position

Thirty buttons, thirty stable colours, built from three facts you already feel in your hands. Hand sets the temperature, column sets the hue, row sets the shade. The same button is the same colour in every song, so the colour of a falling note tells you where to reach before you read a thing.

What this round settles, and doesn't

Next questions

  • How many beats tall is the screen? The grid here shows eight. Fewer beats = bigger, easier-to-read notes but less warning; more beats = more runway but tighter spacing. A per-song or difficulty setting could tune it.
  • Held-note scoring is already built. The engine grades the press, then scales points by the fraction of the beat you actually hold — so the tail isn't decoration, it's the thing being measured. The grid just makes that visible.
  • Chords stack cleanly. Several notes crossing the strike line together light several buttons at once — same as the toy game, just wider.
  • Staggered-track spacing is still the open visual problem: real anglo columns aren't evenly spaced, so two active tracks can sit close. Worth a dedicated pass next.
